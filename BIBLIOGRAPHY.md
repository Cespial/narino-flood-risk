## 2. Climate Characteristics

### 2.1 Precipitation Regimes

Narino has **two fundamentally different precipitation patterns**:

| Zone | Regime | Annual Rainfall | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Andean Region** (~15,466 km^2) | Bimodal | 800-1,600 mm | Wet: Mar-May, Oct-Nov; Dry: Jun-Sep, Dec-Feb |
| **Pacific Region** (~14,754 km^2) | Unimodal | 2,600-5,000+ mm | Wet most of year; slight dip Aug-Sep |

### 2.2 Annual Rainfall by Subregion

| Subregion | Approx. Annual Rainfall (mm) |
|---|---|
| Pacifico Sur (Tumaco) | 2,600 - 3,800 |
| Sanquianga (El Charco, Olaya Herrera) | 3,000 - 5,000 |
| Telembi (Barbacoas) | 3,000 - 4,500 |
| Piedemonte Costero (Mallama, Ricaurte) | 2,000 - 3,500 |
| Centro (Pasto) | 800 - 1,200 |
| Obando (Ipiales) | 800 - 1,100 |
| La Sabana (Tuquerres) | 900 - 1,300 |
| Rio Mayo / Juanambu | 1,000 - 2,000 |
| La Cordillera (Patia canyon) | 800 - 1,500 |

### 2.3 ENSO Impact

**Key finding**: Unlike most Colombian departments, Narino tends to receive **above-normal rainfall during BOTH El Nino and La Nina phases** (20-40% above historical averages). This is unusual and must be discussed in the manuscript.

| Phase | Effect on Narino |
|---|---|
| La Nina | Above-normal rainfall, significantly increased flood/landslide risk |
| El Nino | Paradoxically, also tends to above-normal precipitation (20-40%) |
| Both | Earlier and stronger effects on rivers in western Colombia |

## 3. Flood-Prone Areas and Historical Events

### 3.1 Most Flood-Vulnerable Municipalities

**Critical zone -- Pacific lowlands (Pacifico Sur, Sanquianga, Telembi)**:
- Tumaco -- Mira River overflow, tidal flooding
- Barbacoas -- Telembi River; river rose 14+ meters in one event
- Roberto Payan -- Telembi and Patia River overflow
- Magui (Payan) -- Magui River overflow
- Olaya Herrera -- Sanquianga River; 11,360 affected in single event
- Santa Barbara de Iscuande -- Iscuande River overflow
- El Charco -- Patia River estuary flooding

**Secondary zone -- Andean river valleys**:
- Policarpa -- Patia River canyon flooding
- Mallama -- Landslides and flash floods on steep slopes

Narino is among the departments with the **greatest threat of flash flooding** in Colombia. 36% of all Colombian flood deaths occur in Antioquia, Valle del Cauca, Narino, and Choco combined.

### 3.2 Major Historical Flood Events

| Date | Event | Municipalities | People Affected |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 2017 | 8 rivers overflow simultaneously | Olaya Herrera, Barbacoas, Roberto Payan, Magui, Tumaco, Santa Barbara, Policarpa, Arboleda | ~25,000 |
| Jan 2017 | Mira River overflow | Tumaco | 11,125 |
| Dec 2019 | Telembi River overflow | Barbacoas | ~6,000 families |
| Apr 2021 | Telembi River overflow | Barbacoas | 1,700 families |
| 2010-2011 | La Nina mega-event | Telembi, Magui, Patia basins | Part of 3.5M nationally |
| Feb 2026 | Rain-triggered landslide/flood | Mallama | 7 killed (one family) |

## 4. Terrain Considerations for SAR Analysis

### 4.1 Elevation Zones

| Zone | Elevation | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Pacific Lowlands | 0-200 m | Flat, mangroves, estuaries; best for SAR detection |
| Andean Western Slopes | 200-1,500 m | Steep; high precipitation; high landslide risk |
| Andean Highlands | 1,500-3,500 m | Nudo de los Pastos; bimodal rainfall |
| Volcanic Peaks/Paramos | 3,500-4,764 m | Galeras, Azufral, Cumbal, Chiles volcanoes |
| Eastern Slope | 1,000-300 m | Amazon transition; dense cloud forest |

### 4.2 SAR Detection Challenges

| Challenge | Description | Affected Zones |
|---|---|---|
| Radar shadow | Steep slopes invisible to sensor | Western Andean slopes, volcanic flanks |
| Layover | Hillsides appear "flipped" | Steep Pacific-facing slopes, volcanic cones |
| Foreshortening | Slopes compressed in image | Moderate slopes throughout cordillera |
| Dense vegetation | Canopy attenuates C-band SAR | Pacific lowlands, Telembi, Sanquianga |
| DEM requirements | Accurate DEMs essential for correction | All mountainous terrain |

**Recommendations**:
- Use both ASCENDING and DESCENDING orbits to minimize shadow/layover
- Focus SAR flood detection on flat Pacific lowlands where SAR performs best
- Document SAR accuracy limitations in steep valleys (~60-70% detection)
- HAND and elevation will dominate SHAP importance even more than in Antioquia

## 5. Relevant Academic Literature

### 5.1 Flood Risk and Hydroclimatic Studies

1. **Jaramillo & Hoyos (2013)**. "Impact of the 2010-2011 La Nina phenomenon in Colombia." *Applied Geography*. -- 3.5M affected, US$7.8B damage; Narino among affected departments.

2. **Urrea et al. (2019)**. "Seasonality of Rainfall in Colombia." *Water Resources Research*. -- Identified bimodal (Andean) vs. unimodal (Pacific) regimes in Narino.

3. **Enciso et al. (2020)**. "Teleconnections between Monthly Rainfall Variability and Large-Scale Climate Indices in Southwestern Colombia." *Water (MDPI)*. -- Characterized ENSO, PDO, AMO influence on Narino rainfall.

4. **Arias et al. (2023)**. "Extreme Dry and Wet Events in the Pacific Region of Colombia Estimated in the 21st Century." *Atmosphere (MDPI)*. -- Projected extreme precipitation changes for the Pacific coast.

5. **Riveros-Garzon et al. (2025)**. "Dynamic effect of climate change on flood damage cost in the Andean region of Colombia." *Sustainable Cities and Society*. -- Modeled economic costs of flooding under climate change.

### 5.2 Remote Sensing Studies

6. **Ferrer-Velasco et al. (2020)**. "Drivers of Forest Loss in a Megadiverse Hotspot on the Pacific Coast of Colombia." *Remote Sensing (MDPI)*. -- SAR+optical fusion methodology applicable to Narino's Pacific region.

7. **Accuracy Assessment of Geometric-Distortion Identification Methods for Sentinel-1 SAR in Highland Mountainous Regions (2024)**. *Sensors (PMC)*. -- Directly relevant to Narino's Andean terrain.

8. **Flood Detection with SAR: A Review of Techniques and Datasets (2024)**. *Remote Sensing (MDPI)*. -- Comprehensive review including tropical terrain challenges.

### 5.3 Climate Change and Vulnerability

9. **Camacho-Diaz et al. (2023)**. "Policy-oriented adaptation strategies for artisanal marine fisheries under climate and non-climate stressors in San Andres de Tumaco, Narino." *Marine Policy*. -- Tumaco vulnerability indices.

10. **Palacios-Moreno et al. (2022)**. "The economic vulnerability of fishing households to climate change in the south Pacific region of Colombia." *PLoS ONE*. -- Socioeconomic vulnerability in Narino's Pacific coast.

11. **Carvajal-Escobar et al. (2022)**. "Monthly Rainfall Anomalies Forecasting for Southwestern Colombia Using Artificial Neural Networks." *Water (MDPI)*. -- Neural network rainfall prediction for Narino.

## 6. Methodological Notes for Narino Replication

### 6.1 Parameters to Adjust

| Parameter | Antioquia Value | Narino Value | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| DEPARTMENT_NAME | 'Antioquia' | 'Narino' | GAUL Level 1 |
| SUBREGIONS | 9 subregions, 125 munis | 13 subregions, 64 munis | Different admin division |
| MAP_CENTER | lat=6.9, lon=-75.6 | lat=1.29, lon=-77.36 | Geographic center |
| MAP_ZOOM | 8 | 8 | Similar scale |
| Expected area | 63,612 km^2 | 33,268 km^2 | Smaller department |
| SAR orbit | DESCENDING | DESCENDING (+ ASCENDING) | Terrain considerations |

### 6.2 GAUL Dataset Notes

- Department name in GAUL Level 1: **'Narino'** (ADM1_CODE = 955, no tilde)
- 'El Tablon' in GAUL = 'El Tablon de Gomez' officially
- 'Magui' in GAUL = 'Magui Payan' officially
- 'Narino' (municipality) and 'El Penol' may be absent from GAUL Level 2

### 6.3 Seasonal Analysis Considerations

The standard bimodal seasonal analysis (DJF, MAM, JJA, SON) applies to the Andean portion of Narino but **does not capture the Pacific coast's near-continuous rainfall**. Consider:
- Running separate seasonal analyses for Andean vs. Pacific subregions
- Adding a "Pacific regime" flag to the climate analysis
- Documenting the limitation in the manuscript
